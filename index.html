The search for Burau's missing kernel

Rules

The game is made up of 9 levels. In each level you are controlling a set of 3x3 matrices over the complex numbers. By moving in one of the four compass directions, you multiply your matrices with another matrix, whose entries depend on the direction and the particular level you are in.

The goal of the game is to find a non-trivial path so that each of your matrices become the identity matrix. The rules are set up so that if you move back and forth, for instance east, then west, then you get back to where you started, but this is not necessarily the case if you move in a full square, say east, north, west, south. If this sounds a bit weird, just think of it as if you are moving around in this graph.

The levels are related. In each level, the matrices are parametrized by a non-zero complex number ğ‘, whose value differs from level to level, and in some levels, multiple values ğ‘ are used at the same time. About

The matrices as given above are taken from this paper. It is an open problem in mathematics to determine whether a non-trivial path which takes you back to the identity matrix for any given value of ğ‘ exists. In this game, we attack the less ambitious problem of trying to find paths for fixed values of ğ‘. Nevertheless, I am not familiar with solutions to the final levels of the game, and so finding those is the same as pushing the frontier of mathematics.

It being impossible to find a path that works for any given value of ğ‘ is equivalent to the open problem that the so-called Burau representation of the braid group on four strands is faithful. First studied in the 1930s, besides making an appearance in the group theory of the braid groups, the Burau representation also shows up in quantum field theory, where it acts on the space of states for a certain 2-dimensional disk with defects. They also make an appearance in quantum computing, where the matrices show up as quantum gates.

The neat thing is that you don't need to worry about what any of those words actually mean. To understand the problem, all you need to understand is multiplication of complex matrices, which is something that is generally taught at high school or first year undergrad level mathematics.
